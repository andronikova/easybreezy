EasyBreezy is made to help you easily distribute your monthly income following 3 simple steps:

1. Fill in your income and if necessary update your monthly expenses and current values of your accounts.

2. EasyBreezy distributes your money using its algorithm.

3. (optional) You can correct payments by hand if needed and save all results to history, if you would like.


Types of accounts There are three types of accounts in EasyBreezy: expenses, savings and goals. You can have unlimited number of accounts of each type. Table below shows main properties of account types.

Expenses are the payments with fixed monthly value. This type of account is suitable for apartment rent, electricity bills, credit payments. It is also recommended to use this type of account for monthly allowance (your everyday expenses for food, hobbies, and other personal stuff)


Saving accounts are for saving up some money. They correspond to regular payments which are defined as a fraction of your income. For example, you can save 10% of your income to gather nest-egg for retiremenet. You could also use saving accounts to make some emergency savings for job loss or medical bills. In any case you should put an upper limit for your savings, so monthly payments stop as soon as you reach this value. In nest-egg savings you may wish to put your upper limit as high as you can imagine.

We ask you to choose one reserve account among your savings. This account is used in case your income doesn't cover your expenses. See section "account priority" to read about this situation.


Goal accounts can be used for saving up for large scheduled expenses. You should provide the date and the amount of money you would like to have by the date. Monthly payment is calculated by taking into account missing amount (equal to goal value minus already saved value) and the deadline. For example, you can use goal account to save up for summer vacation, planned reconstruction works or for some expensive purchase, e.g. car, iPhone.
Account priority

Account priority is important if your income is not enough to cover all your payments. In this case you should choose which accounts to replenish first.

EasyBreezy considers expenses accounts to have the highest priority since you always must pay the bills. Savings have intermediate priority. Goals have the lowest priority since they are less important for your survival.

If your income doesn't cover all your expenses, money from the reserve account are used (Fig. 1). In this situation there are no replenishes for saving or goal accounts.

If your income does cover all your expenses, the saving accounts are replenished (Fig. 2). If residue is less than total sum of savings payments, it is distributed between all savings in proportion.

Goals are replenished if your income covers all your expenses and all savings. If residue is not enough to cover all goals, it is distributed between all goals in proportion (Fig. 3).

It may happen that after all you still have non-zero residue (Fig. 4). In this case you may add it to any account you wish.

Example of usage

Peter just started to use EasyBreezy. He creates two expenses accounts. The first one is for the rent, which amounts for 30 rubles. The second one is his monthly allowance, which he estimates as 40 rubles.

As Peter wants to die rich, he sets up a saving account for retirement, planning to save up 7% of his income. He uses another saving account for emergency fund, fearing he does not get paid next month. Peter puts 8% of his income to this account, hoping to amass at least 140 rubles. This sum would allow him to live without salary for 2 months.

He also creates a goal account, planning to spend 120 rubles in 1 year for summer vacation.

Next day Peter gets his payment. He works two jobs with salaries of 40 and 60 rubles. Thus, his income is 100 rubles. EasyBreezy calculates that he must pay 30 and 40 rubles to his expenses accounts, 7 and 8 rubles to his saving account (7% and 8% of 100 rubles income) and 10 rubles for his goal account (120 rubles spread over 12 months).

For the residual 5 rubles Peter buys Happy Meal to feel better for himself.
